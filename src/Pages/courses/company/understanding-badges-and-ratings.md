# Understanding Badges and Ratings: A Guide for Companies

This short guide explains how members prove their skills on Ascivan, so you can read a profile and hire with confidence. Ascivan runs on one idea: proof over pedigree. People here earn their standing by doing real project work, not by listing credentials, and once you know how to read that proof, a profile tells you far more than a resume does. Work through the sections in order.

---

## Section 1: Why Proof Over Pedigree

**Goal:** Understand what problem badges solve, so the rest of this guide makes sense.

A resume tells you what someone says they can do. A badge tells you what they have already done, on a real project, reviewed by the people they did it with. That is the difference Ascivan is built around.

Every badge on the platform was earned by contributing to a real project in a specific role. The member did the work, delivered something, and had that contribution assessed, so nothing on a profile is self-awarded. When you see a badge, you are reading a record of completed, reviewed work, which removes most of the guesswork that makes early-career hiring risky.

A badge is not a certificate from a course, and it is not a test score. Someone can watch a hundred tutorials and still have zero badges, because badges only come from shipping work with a team. A member with three badges has finished three real projects, which is a stronger signal than any number of finished courses.

---

## Section 2: The Six Tracks

**Goal:** Learn the six skill areas, so you can match a member to the work you need done.

Every badge belongs to one of six tracks. Each track is a family of related tech roles, and the track tells you the kind of work a member has proven they can do:

- **TechDev** (Coding Developer): writes and ships software across frontend, backend, mobile, and full-stack.
- **TechArchs** (Low/No-Code Developer): builds working products on no-code and low-code platforms, and automates workflows.
- **TechQA** (Quality Tester): ensures software quality through testing, reviews, and quality control.
- **TechGuard** (Network and Cybersecurity): protects systems and networks, and runs cloud administration and DevOps.
- **TechPO** (Product / Project Owner): owns product vision, requirements, and the backlog, and steers projects to completion.
- **TechLeads** (Non-Technical Roles): fills delivery and non-coding roles such as project management, technical writing, and research.

A member can hold badges in more than one track. Someone with TechDev and TechLeads badges has proven both hands-on building and delivery skills, which is the kind of range a small team often needs.

---

## Section 3: Reading Badge Levels

**Goal:** Learn what the level on a badge means, so you can gauge depth, not just presence.

Badges within a track add up to a level, and that level reflects how much proven work a member has in that track, not how long they have been a member. As a member completes more projects in a track, their level in that track rises, so a higher level means more delivered and reviewed work in that specific area.

A member can be advanced in one track and a beginner in another, and that is normal and useful information. Level tells you depth: for a small, well-scoped task, an eager lower-level member who reviewed well can be ideal, while for something you cannot afford to get wrong, you should weight the level in the exact track the work falls under.

Always pair the level with the track. An advanced TechQA member is a strong tester, but that says nothing about their coding depth, so match the level to the track that fits your need. This is the most common misread: a high level is only high for the track it sits in.

---

## Section 4: Reading Ratings and Contribution Quality

**Goal:** Learn what the ratings and contribution notes on a badge tell you about how someone works.

Each badge can carry a record of how well the member contributed on that project, along with their role. A badge records whether a member's contribution was rated as excellent, good, or fair, and this is feedback from real project work, not a popularity score. Two members can both hold a TechDev badge, but their contribution quality tells you who carried more of the work and delivered it well.

Members who reach a certain level can also teach others by publishing lessons, and those lessons get rated by the community. A member with highly rated lessons has proven they understand a skill well enough to explain it, which is a strong signal of both mastery and communication.

---

## Section 5: Putting It Together to Hire With Confidence

**Goal:** Combine everything above into a simple way to read any profile.

You now have every piece, and here is a reliable order for reading a profile:

1. Look at which tracks the member holds badges in. This tells you the kind of work they can do.
2. Look at the level in the track you care about. This tells you how much proven depth they have there.
3. Look at the number of badges and their contribution quality. This tells you how consistently they deliver.
4. Look at any community teaching ratings. This tells you how well they understand and communicate the skill.

In under a minute you can move from a name to a grounded, evidence-based read on whether someone fits your project. When a profile fits, you can post a job or invite the member to a project directly, and the same badge system then records how they perform for you, which keeps the whole platform honest over time.
