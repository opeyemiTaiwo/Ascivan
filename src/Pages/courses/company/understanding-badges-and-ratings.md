# Understanding Badges and Ratings: A Guide for Companies

This short guide explains how members prove their skills on Ascivan, so you can read a profile and hire with confidence. Ascivan runs on one idea: proof over pedigree. People here earn their standing by doing real project work, not by listing credentials. Once you know how to read that proof, a profile tells you far more than a resume does.

Work through the sections in order. By the end you will be able to look at any member and know exactly what their badges, levels, and ratings are telling you.

---

## Section 1: Why proof over pedigree

**Goal:** Understand what problem badges solve, so the rest of this guide makes sense.

A resume tells you what someone says they can do. A badge tells you what they have already done, on a real project, reviewed by the people they did it with. That is the difference Ascivan is built around.

**What a badge represents.**
Every badge on the platform was earned by contributing to a real project in a specific role. The member did the work, delivered something, and had that contribution assessed. Nothing on a profile is self-awarded.

*Why this matters to you:* when you see a badge, you are not reading a claim. You are reading a record of completed, reviewed work. That removes most of the guesswork that makes early-career hiring risky.

**What a badge is not.**
A badge is not a certificate from a course, and it is not a test score. Someone can watch a hundred tutorials and still have zero badges, because badges only come from shipping work with a team.

*Why this matters to you:* a member with three badges has finished three real projects. That is a stronger signal than any number of finished courses.

## Section 2: The six tracks

**Goal:** Learn the six skill areas, so you can match a member to the work you need done.

Every badge belongs to one of six tracks. Each track is a family of related tech roles. When you read a profile, the track tells you the kind of work the member has proven they can do.

**The tracks:**
- **TechDev** (Coding Developer): writes and ships software across frontend, backend, mobile, and full-stack.
- **TechArchs** (Low/No-Code Developer): builds working products on no-code and low-code platforms, and automates workflows.
- **TechQA** (Quality Tester): ensures software quality through testing, reviews, and quality control.
- **TechGuard** (Network and Cybersecurity): protects systems and networks, and runs cloud administration and DevOps.
- **TechPO** (Product / Project Owner): owns product vision, requirements, and the backlog, and steers projects to completion.
- **TechLeads** (Non-Technical Roles): fills delivery and non-coding roles such as project management, technical writing, and research.

*Why this matters to you:* a member can hold badges in more than one track. Someone with TechDev and TechLeads badges has proven both hands-on building and delivery skills, which is exactly the kind of range a small team needs.

## Section 3: Reading badge levels

**Goal:** Learn what the level on a badge means, so you can gauge depth, not just presence.

Badges within a track add up to a level. The level reflects how much proven work a member has in that track, not how long they have been a member.

**The levels, from the ground up.**
As a member completes more projects in a track, their level in that track rises. A higher level means more delivered work and more reviewed contributions in that specific area. A member can be advanced in one track and a beginner in another, and that is normal and useful information.

*Why this matters to you:* level tells you depth. For a small, well-scoped task, a lower-level member who is eager and reviewed well can be ideal. For something you cannot afford to get wrong, weight the level in the exact track the work falls under.

**Read the level in context of the track.**
Always pair the level with the track. An advanced TechQA member is a strong tester, but that says nothing about their coding depth. Match the level to the track that fits your need.

*Why this matters to you:* this is the most common misread. A high level is only high for the track it sits in.

---

## Section 4: Reading ratings and contribution quality

**Goal:** Learn what the ratings and contribution notes on a badge tell you about how someone works.

Each badge can carry a record of how well the member contributed on that project: an assessment of the quality of their work, and their role. Some members also earn ratings by teaching other members through community lessons.

**Contribution quality.**
A badge records whether a member's contribution to that project was rated as excellent, good, or fair. This is feedback from real project work, not a popularity score.

*Why this matters to you:* two members can both hold a TechDev badge, but their contribution quality tells you who carried more of the work and delivered it well.

**Community ratings.**
Members who reach a certain level can teach others by publishing lessons, and those lessons get rated by the community. A member with highly rated lessons has proven they understand a skill well enough to explain it.

*Why this matters to you:* the ability to teach a skill is a strong signal of mastery and of communication ability, both of which matter on a team.

---

## Section 5: Putting it together to hire with confidence

**Goal:** Combine everything above into a simple way to read any profile.

You now have every piece. Here is how to read a profile quickly and well.

**A simple reading order.**
1. Look at which tracks the member holds badges in. This tells you the kind of work they can do.
2. Look at the level in the track you care about. This tells you how much proven depth they have there.
3. Look at the number of badges and their contribution quality. This tells you how consistently they deliver.
4. Look at any community teaching ratings. This tells you how well they understand and communicate the skill.

*Why this matters to you:* in under a minute you can move from a name to a grounded, evidence-based read on whether someone fits your project.

**What to do next.**
When a profile fits, you can post a job or invite the member to a project directly. The same badge system then records how they perform for you, which keeps the whole platform honest over time.

*Why this matters to you:* every hire you make adds to the shared record, so the signal you relied on stays trustworthy for the next company too.
